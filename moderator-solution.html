Q1 – Which bridge item carries the most uncertainty, and how would you handle it in the SPA?

Pension provisions are typically the highest-uncertainty item: the deficit can widen post-signing depending on actuarial assumptions and investment returns. Recommended mechanism: escrow (seller retains a defined amount in trust until an actuarial review at closing confirms the liability) or W&I insurance to cap seller exposure. Alternatively, a price adjustment clause tied to a post-closing actuarial valuation. NWC is also a valid answer: the high 2022 inventory level creates risk if the backlog is not fully normalized — a locked box with a normalized NWC peg is the cleanest solution.

Q2 – Under what conditions would you propose an earn-out?

Earn-out makes sense when buyer and seller disagree on the forward earnings trajectory — e.g., LORCH's FY 2023e EBITDA of EUR k 5,440 (+114% vs. 2022) may be too optimistic for the buyer to underwrite fully. Typical structure: tie earn-out to EBITDA target over 2 years post-closing (e.g., EUR k 5,000 in FY 2024). Seller-side risks: (1) buyer controls operations post-closing and may cut investments that depress short-term EBITDA; (2) accounting disputes around what counts as "EBITDA"; (3) earn-out creates continued dependency rather than a clean exit. Best paired with clear definitions in the SPA and a minimum floor payment.

Module 6

Discussion Questions – Model Answers

1) Who pays more: strategic buyer or financial investor?

Strategic buyer typically pays more. Reasons: (a) can realize operational synergies (shared procurement, manufacturing overlap, combined sales channels) that create value beyond standalone; (b) can justify a higher entry multiple because the synergy EBITDA upside lowers their effective purchase multiple post-integration; (c) LORCH's Lorch Connect IoT platform and Yaskawa cobot partnership are attractive for automation-focused strategic buyers (e.g., LECO, ESAB). Financial investors (PE) are constrained by IRR targets, debt service capacity (leverage ratio ×3–4×), and exit multiple assumptions — price is capped by LBO math, not synergies.

2) Does EBITDA or multiple sensitivity influence price more?

EBITDA is the more powerful lever at this company size. Price = EBITDA × Multiple. A 1× multiple change on EUR k 3,483 = EUR k 3,483 impact. A 10% EBITDA improvement = EUR k 348 × 11.5× = EUR k 4,005 impact. The multiplicative effect of EBITDA means documentation quality (EBITDA adjustments) and forward-looking earnings credibility often matter more than haggling over the multiple. That said: in a competitive auction, multiple compression/expansion between bidders is the key variable.

3) Which two risks would you reflect directly in the price?

  • Customer/market concentration: Russia exit wiped EUR k 172 EBITDA. New geographic concentration risk (Eastern Europe dependency) → buyer will price in a risk discount or escrow.
  • Key-person / owner-operator risk: Grüb brothers management. No documented succession, no professional management layer fully in place. PE buyers will request management equity plan or retention arrangement → effective price reduction for seller.
  • Also acceptable: OEM/automation dependency on Yaskawa (single-partner risk for cobot revenue), or inventory quality risk (elevated WIP/FG, slow-mover risk in backlog).

4) Which single pre-sale measure increases value the fastest?

Recommended: EBITDA normalization and documentation quality. A clean, auditor-reviewed quality of earnings (QoE) report reducing information asymmetry directly increases the multiple buyers are willing to pay — buyers price uncertainty via a risk discount. Documenting the EUR k 942 adjustment with third-party evidence transforms EBITDA from EUR k 2,541 to EUR k 3,483: that alone at 11.5× = EUR 10.8m of additional equity value. Secondary option: accelerate Lorch Connect revenue recognition — recurring IoT/SaaS revenues command a premium multiple and broaden the buyer universe to digital-industrial acquirers.

Module 6 – Closing

Process Recommendation & Value Creation

Recommended sale process

Structured limited auction (4–6 bidders): Broad public auction would expose LORCH to competitor intelligence risk and management distraction. Full bilateral (direct outreach) sacrifices price tension. A limited auction with pre-screened strategic buyers (LECO, ESAB, Fronius, potentially Atlas Copco/Epiroc) plus 2–3 PE firms (with automation focus) balances price maximization and process control. Timeline: ~6 months from NDA to SPA. Seller advisory role: prepare clean IM, QoE, management presentation, and data room. Aim for binding bids from 3+ parties.

Top 3 value-creation measures before exit

  1. Quality of Earnings (QoE) documentation — Commission a Big-4 or specialist M&A QoE report confirming all EBITDA adjustments with third-party evidence. Directly translates into a higher defensible EBITDA and reduces buyer risk discount on the multiple.
  2. Lorch Connect revenue packaging — Formalize IoT subscription / SaaS fee structure for Lorch Connect and cobot monitoring. Even EUR k 300–500 of recurring revenue shifts EBITDA quality perception. Recurring-revenue components attract digital acquirers and compress the applicable multiple upward.
  3. NWC normalization & inventory audit — Work down the peak inventory (FG + WIP) to near-normalized levels before the transaction date. This reduces NWC surplus risk in the price negotiation and removes the slow-mover discount argument from buyers during due diligence.
Instructor note: The football field for LORCH sits approximately EUR 23m–52m EV (full range) with a mid-case equity value of approximately EUR 39m. The wide range reflects the fundamental challenge of valuing a mid-market, family-owned industrial company with an inflecting EBITDA trajectory. Disciplined peer selection and adjustment quality are the two areas where students can most improve their result.
